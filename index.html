Results-Driven Development

What?

This is a development workflow guideline for project leaders. With this development workflow your team will be able to:

  • Identify High Value Issues
  • Identify when they are stuck and what to do
  • Build Features Faster
  • Produce Evolution-Ready Code

For this workflow to be successful, you MUST HAVE only one (or less) process for each problem the process is trying to resolve. Here we are trying to combine the least amount of processes to solve the most amount of issues that are commonly found during project management.

What you need to do

  • Run a Design Sprint to identify product features and due dates
  • Build a delivery pipeline to quickly show progress to the customer
  • Build a learning hub to keep all developers programming using similar development patterns
  • Enforce real collaboration to maximize everyone's strengths

Run a Design Sprint

There are many ways to do Design Sprints. How you do it depends on the project's time and budget. Although I highly recommend Google Design Sprints, they can be unnecessarily long. It's more important to understand the goal of the Design Sprint and build a custom approach than following the guide step by step.

So what matters? A Design Sprint needs to answer these 4 key questions:

  • What is the problem you are solving? (Problem)
  • How are you solving it? (Strategy)
  • What milestones do you need to achieve? (Tactics)
  • What does the solution look like? (Objectives)
Here's an example

Suppose you own a shop and you want to reduce costs by improving inventory management

Step 1: Problem

What is the problem you are solving? (Problem)

I can improve inventory management by:

  • reducing time spent when I interact with my inventory (restocking and purchase orders)
  • reducing the amount of resources I use to keep track of my inventory (people spent restocking, counting, or receiving orders)
I choose to reduce time spent
Step 2: Strategy

How are you solving it? (Strategy)

I can reduce time spent on my inventories by:

  • increasing speed of interactions with inventories
  • decreasing visits to inventories
I choose to increase the speed of interactions

Step 3: Tactics

What milestones do you need to achieve? (Tactics)

To increase the speed of interactions with inventories I need to

  • organize my inventories and reflect this organization on a system so that anyone that understands the system can immediately understand the inventory
  • synchronize sales to restock orders so less time is spent counting store inventory
  • synchronize purchase orders to inventory so less time is spent organizing warehouse inventory
I choose all 3 tactics
Step 4: Objectives

What does the solution look like? (Objectives)

You need to have everyone involved draw the application. I suggest folding a paper into 8 parts, each one being a screen. After drawing, everyone shares their vision of the app and how they enforced tactics. After reviewing all the solutions, someone needs to make the decisions on which parts to keep.

How does the Design Sprint translate into issues?

The objectives are going to produce all the issues for our github project. There are 2 kinds of issues:

  • Useful Issues
  • Useless Issues
  • Useful Issues are issues that have a direct and measurable impact on features.
  • Useless Issues are issues that improve the developers quality of life.

The only kinds of issues we want to create are Useful Issues. There is only one useless issue that matters when you're starting a project: Building the delivery pipeline (we'll talk about this later).

Issues need to be specific actions. They should always start with a verb (because you are doing something). Here is an exampe of a good issue vs a bad one:

Good!
Build navbar component
Bad!
Inventory Page

Notice how the good issue is a specific atom, molecule, organism, template, or page inside the app. The good issue also begins with a verb. Nouns fail to explain the context of what needs to be done.
